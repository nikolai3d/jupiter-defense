The science: slingshots, shields, and honest caveats

1 · The twin-system experiment

Both halves of the screen run the same physics on the same comets — identical positions, velocities, and random seed. The only difference: the left system has a giant planet on a circular orbit at 5.2 AU, the right one doesn’t. Each comet feels the Sun and (on the left) Jupiter; the comets are numerous enough to be treated as massless test particles, so this is the classic restricted three-body problem, integrated honestly — no scripted outcomes. Every divergence between the two ledgers is Jupiter’s doing.

2 · How a giant planet clears the yard

A comet that crosses Jupiter’s orbit will, sooner or later, pass close to it. Each close pass is a gravitational slingshot that trades energy between comet and planet. The kicks are random in sign, so the comet’s orbital energy random-walks — and the walk has absorbing ends: enough gained energy and the comet is ejected to interstellar space (watch the pale violet dots leave); enough lost and its orbit decouples from Earth’s. A few unlucky ones hit the planet itself, as Shoemaker–Levy 9 famously did in 1994. Either way, an Earth-crossing comet in the left world has a short life expectancy, while the same comet on the right circulates for eternity, re-rolling its impact dice every perihelion. That is the whole “shield”: not a wall, but a bouncer with a fast eviction policy.

3 · Friend and foe — the honest caveat

The popular claim (“without Jupiter, impacts would be 1,000× more common” — Wetherill 1994) is real science but not the whole story. Horner & Jones (2008–2012) re-ran the question across planet masses and found Jupiter is a Jekyll-and-Hyde neighbour: it ejects comets, yes, but it also nudges asteroids out of the main belt and flings Oort-cloud visitors inward — and an intermediate-mass giant (a Saturn) can be worse than nothing. This simulation shows the population where the shield story is strongest: comets already on Jupiter-crossing, Earth-threatening orbits. Try the “Inner rogue belt” family — Jupiter can’t reach it directly, and the shield largely switches off. If the verdict panel ever tells you Jupiter is losing, that’s not a bug; it’s Hyde.

4 · What we inflated, and why it’s fair

A real Earth is 4×10⁻⁵ AU across — with a few thousand comets you’d wait years of wall-clock time for one honest hit. Here Earth’s capture radius is 0.004 AU (~75× its gravitationally-focused radius) and Jupiter’s is 0.01 AU (~2× its focused cross-section); the Sun swallows anything inside 0.02 AU, standing in for the tidal shredding of sungrazers. The inflation is applied identically in both worlds, so the ratio of impact counts — the number this page exists to measure — is meaningful even though the absolute rate is accelerated. Likewise the clock: real Jupiter-family comets take ~10⁵ years to be evicted; at a few hundred simulated years per second you can watch it happen over coffee.

Numerical method: adaptive velocity-Verlet (kick–drift–kick with re-evaluated acceleration), per-comet timestep dt = η·min(r☉^{3/2}, r♃^{3/2}/√m♃) with η = 0.035, further clamped near Earth so encounters are resolved below the capture radius — no tunnelling through the target. Planets ride an exact analytic two-body solution (the Sun visibly orbits the barycenter), so planetary motion carries zero integration error. The CPU engine runs float64; the GPU engine runs the identical scheme in float32 fragment shaders, advancing every comet hundreds of substeps per frame entirely on the GPU — possible because test particles never interact, an O(N) problem instead of the O(N²) of self-gravity. Comets that can’t finish a frame’s time budget carry a private time debt and catch up next frame, so slow hardware degrades speed, never physics.

Initial conditions: perihelia drawn uniformly inside/near Earth’s orbit, aphelia log-uniform per family, Rayleigh-distributed inclinations, all other angles uniform. Units: G = 1, M☉ = 1, 1 length unit = 1 AU (1 M♃ = 9.54×10⁻⁴ M☉); one year = 2π time units. Escape is declared past 60 AU, or beyond 30 AU with positive orbital energy.

What’s not modeled: Earth’s own gravity (its inflated radius stands in for focusing), the other planets, planetary migration, the Yarkovsky effect, and comet fading. Saturn matters in reality — it hands comets down to Jupiter — so treat this as the cleanest possible A/B test of one giant planet, not a full solar-system ephemeris.
